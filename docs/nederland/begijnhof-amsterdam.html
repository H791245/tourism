Story of this place

Begijnhof, one of Amsterdam's oldest and most serene hofjes (almshouses), holds a rich history stretching back to the 14th century. It was founded by the Begijnes, a Catholic sisterhood of lay religious women who lived together in community without taking formal vows, dedicating their lives to prayer, charity, and often a degree of independence. Unlike other religious institutions in the city, the Amsterdam Begijnhof was unique as it was established as a singular, enclosed complex, rather than being part of a larger monastery. Its existence reflects a fascinating chapter in the city‚Äôs religious past. During the Reformation in the 16th century, when Catholicism was outlawed in the Netherlands, the Begijnhof faced significant challenges. While many Catholic properties were confiscated, the Beguines were, remarkably, permitted to remain in their homes. This was partly due to the private nature of their community and their steadfast refusal to leave. However, their main chapel was seized by the city authorities and converted into a Dutch Reformed church, which today serves as the English Reformed Church. Undeterred, the Beguines adapted by creating a 'schuilkerk' or hidden church within two of their houses ‚Äì the H.H. Joannes en Catharina ‚Äì where they could continue to practice their faith in secret. This clandestine church became a vital spiritual haven for them for centuries. The Begijnhof continued to house Beguines for hundreds of years. The last true Begijne, Sister Antonia, passed away in 1971, marking the end of a centuries-long tradition of this unique community. Today, the houses are primarily occupied by single women, respecting the original spirit of the place. It remains a tranquil, verdant oasis, offering a profound sense of peace and historical continuity amidst the bustling modern city centre, a living testament to resilience and faith.
