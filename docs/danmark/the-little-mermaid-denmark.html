Story of this place

The iconic Little Mermaid statue, perched gracefully on a rock in Copenhagen's harbor, owes its existence to Danish fairy tale author Hans Christian Andersen, who published "The Little Mermaid" in 1837. The beloved story captured the imagination of many, including Carl Jacobsen, the son of the founder of Carlsberg Breweries. Deeply moved by a ballet performance based on Andersen's tale at the Royal Danish Theatre in 1909, Jacobsen was inspired to commission a sculpture that would immortalize the poignant character. He enlisted sculptor Edvard Eriksen for the task. Eriksen created the bronze statue, taking inspiration for the mermaid's head from ballerina Ellen Price, who danced the lead role in the ballet. However, Price declined to pose nude, so Eriksen's wife, Eline Eriksen, famously modeled for the mermaid's body. The statue, depicting the mermaid gazing longingly at the shore, was completed and gifted to the city of Copenhagen by Jacobsen. Unveiled on August 23, 1913, at the Langelinie promenade, the statue quickly became a symbol of Copenhagen and one of Denmark's most famous landmarks. Despite its seemingly tranquil presence, The Little Mermaid has endured a tumultuous history marked by numerous acts of vandalism. She has been decapitated twice (in 1964 and 1998), had an arm severed, been painted various colors, and even been blasted off her rock and into the water. Each time, however, she has been painstakingly restored, steadfastly returning to her contemplative perch, much to the delight of millions of tourists who visit her annually. Her enduring presence makes her a poignant testament to the resilience of art and a beloved cultural icon.
