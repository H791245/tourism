Mausoleum of Mohammed V, Rabat, Morocco

Highlights

Did you know?

A stunning example of modern Moroccan architecture, the mausoleum features intricate zellige tilework, hand-carved cedarwood ceilings, and white marble, designed by Vietnamese architect Eric Vo Toan.

Did you know?

It serves as the final resting place for three members of the Moroccan royal family: King Mohammed V, his son King Hassan II, and his other son Prince Moulay Abdellah.

Did you know?

While primarily a mausoleum, the complex also functions as an active mosque. Non-Muslim visitors are welcome to view the tombs from a designated gallery but are asked to respect prayer times and dress codes.

Did you know?

The mausoleum is guarded by ceremonial royal guards, often on horseback, dressed in traditional Moroccan attire, adding to the site's regal atmosphere.

Story of this place

The Mausoleum of Mohammed V in Rabat stands as a profound symbol of Morocco's independence and a cornerstone of its modern history. Construction commenced in 1961, following the passing of King Mohammed V, and concluded a decade later in 1971. King Mohammed V (born Sidi Mohammed ben Youssef) is deeply revered as the architect of modern Morocco, having led the nation's struggle for independence from the French Protectorate, a goal achieved in 1956. His staunch refusal to bow to colonial pressures, particularly during the turbulent 1950s, led to his exile, first to Corsica and then Madagascar. This act, however, inadvertently transformed him into a powerful national symbol, uniting the Moroccan people in their quest for self-determination. Upon his triumphant return, he guided the newly independent kingdom. The mausoleum was commissioned by his son and successor, King Hassan II, as an eternal tribute to his father's monumental legacy. Designed by the Vietnamese architect Eric Vo Toan, the structure is a magnificent embodiment of traditional Moroccan design, meticulously executed with pristine white marble, distinctive green tiled roofs symbolizing Islam, and an interior adorned with intricate zellige tilework, hand-carved cedarwood ceilings, and elaborate stucco details. It serves as the final resting place for King Mohammed V, his son King Hassan II (who passed in 1999), and his other son Prince Moulay Abdellah. Positioned opposite the historic Hassan Tower and the ruins of its accompanying mosque, the mausoleum is not only a royal necropolis but also an active mosque, underscoring its enduring spiritual relevance and making it a place of both historical reflection and active worship.
