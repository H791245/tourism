Story of this place

The Kasbah of the Udayas, perched majestically at the mouth of the Bou Regreg river in Rabat, boasts a rich and complex history spanning several centuries. Its origins trace back to the 12th century when the Almohad Caliph Abd al-Mu'min built a ribat (fortified monastery) here, intended as a base for his jihad against Spain and a defensive stronghold. Later, his successor, Yaqub al-Mansur, transformed it into a formidable city, constructing the monumental Bab Oudaya gate and a mosque, envisioning it as the capital of his empire before eventually moving to Marrakech. Following the collapse of the Almohad Empire, the Kasbah fell into relative disuse for several centuries, suffering from neglect and various sieges. Its fortunes revived dramatically in the 17th century with the arrival of Moriscos, Muslim and Jewish refugees expelled from Andalusia. These skilled artisans and traders revitalized the neglected fortress, transforming it into a vibrant town and, infamously, the heart of the short-lived Republic of Sal√©, a notorious pirate haven that preyed on European shipping lanes. The Udaya tribe, an Arab warrior group, was later settled here by Sultan Moulay Ismail in the 18th century to defend Rabat and provide security, giving the kasbah its current name. During the Alaouite dynasty, particularly under Sultan Moulay Abdallah in the 18th century, the Kasbah underwent significant restoration and beautification, including the creation of the picturesque Andalusian Garden. Today, the Kasbah Oudaya stands as a living testament to Rabat‚Äôs layered past, a blend of Almohad grandeur, Andalusian charm, and pirate intrigue. It was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2012, recognized for its outstanding universal value as a well-preserved example of a North African ribat and its unique urban fabric. Its narrow, blue-and-white alleys continue to draw visitors into its captivating narrative.
