Pantheon, Rome

The Pantheon is centrally located in Rome's historic Pigna district, gracing the picturesque Piazza della Rotonda. The piazza itself is a bustling hub, filled with outdoor cafes, restaurants, and street performers, creating a vibrant atmosphere. From the Pantheon, visitors are within easy walking distance of several other iconic Roman landmarks, including the elegant Piazza Navona (a 5-minute walk), the famous Trevi Fountain (about a 10-minute walk), and the historic Largo di Torre Argentina. The surrounding narrow cobblestone streets are charming to explore, offering a genuine taste of old Rome.

Highlights

Did you know?

The Pantheon's dome is the largest unreinforced concrete dome in the world, with a diameter of 43.3 meters (142 feet).

Did you know?

The oculus, the 8.2-meter (27-foot) wide opening at the top of the dome, is the only source of natural light inside the building.

Did you know?

Despite the oculus, the interior of the Pantheon does not typically flood during rainstorms due to a slightly convex floor and an efficient drainage system.

Did you know?

The building's impressive proportions mean its height (to the oculus) is exactly equal to its internal diameter, creating a perfect sphere within.

Did you know?

The inscription on the front, 'M. AGRIPPA L. F. COS. TERTIUM FECIT,' translates to 'Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius, made this when consul for the third time,' referring to the original Pantheon.

Story of this place

The Pantheon stands today as one of the best-preserved ancient Roman buildings and a testament to the engineering prowess of the Roman Empire. Its history stretches back to around 27-25 BC, when Marcus Agrippa, son-in-law of Emperor Augustus, built the first Pantheon, likely as a temple dedicated to all Roman gods. This original structure was largely destroyed by fire in 80 AD. The current magnificent edifice was constructed under the reign of Emperor Hadrian, likely completed around 126 AD. Hadrian chose to retain Agrippa's original inscription on the portico, which often leads to the misconception that Agrippa built the present structure. This new Pantheon was a revolutionary architectural achievement, particularly for its colossal unreinforced concrete dome ‚Äì still the largest of its kind in the world. The dome culminates in a central open oculus, the building's sole source of natural light and ventilation, symbolizing a connection to the heavens. After the decline of the Roman Empire, the Pantheon faced potential ruin like many ancient structures. However, in 609 AD, Byzantine Emperor Phocas gifted the building to Pope Boniface IV, who consecrated it as a Christian church, Santa Maria ad Martyres (St. Mary and the Martyrs). This conversion was pivotal, ensuring its continuous use and preservation through the centuries, saving it from being quarried for materials, a fate that befell many other Roman temples. From the Renaissance onwards, the Pantheon became a revered burial site for prominent Italians. It houses the tombs of the famous artist Raphael, as well as two unified Kings of Italy, Vittorio Emanuele II and Umberto I, and his consort Queen Margherita. Today, it serves as both a church and a monumental tourist attraction, continuously inspiring architects, artists, and visitors with its enduring beauty and profound history.
