Story of this place

Azadi Square, with its iconic Azadi Tower, stands as one of Tehran's most significant and recognizable landmarks. Originally known as Shahyad Tower (King's Memorial Tower), it was commissioned by Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, the last Shah of Iran, to commemorate the 2,500th anniversary of the Persian Empire. The design was the result of a competition won by a young Bah√° º√≠ architect, Hossein Amanat, in the 1960s. Amanat's vision masterfully blended elements of Sassanian and Islamic architecture with modern design principles, creating a powerful symbol that bridged ancient Persian heritage with contemporary aspirations. Constructed from 8,000 blocks of white marble from Isfahan, the tower reaches a height of 45 meters, its distinctive arches and geometric patterns reflecting intricate Persian artistry. The tower was inaugurated on October 16, 1971, and quickly became a focal point for national celebrations and state events. However, its purpose dramatically shifted following the 1979 Islamic Revolution. Renamed Azadi Tower (Freedom Tower) and the surrounding area Azadi Square (Freedom Square), it transformed from a monument of the monarchy into a symbol of the revolution and national freedom. Since then, Azadi Square has served as the primary venue for many of Iran's largest demonstrations, national rallies, and annual celebrations, including the anniversary of the revolution. Its historical journey mirrors the profound changes Iran has undergone, making it not just an architectural marvel but a living testament to the nation's evolving identity and spirit.
