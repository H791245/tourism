The best time to visit Imam Reza Shrine and Mashhad is during spring (March to May) and early autumn (September to November). During these seasons, the weather is mild and pleasant, making it comfortable for exploring the vast complex and the city. Summers (June to August) can be very hot, and winters (December to February) can be very cold, with potential for snow.

Story of this place

The Imam Reza Shrine in Mashhad, Iran, stands as the holiest site in Iran for Shia Muslims and one of the largest mosque complexes in the world. Its history is deeply intertwined with the life and martyrdom of Ali ibn Musa al-Reza (Imam Reza), the eighth Imam of Twelver Shia Islam. Imam Reza was invited to Marv (present-day Turkmenistan) by the Abbasid Caliph Ma'mun in 816 CE. He was reportedly martyred in 818 CE in Sanabad, a village that later became Mashhad ('place of martyrdom'). Initially, Imam Reza was buried alongside Caliph Harun al-Rashid in a humble tomb. Over the centuries, this modest burial site transformed into the magnificent complex seen today. Early structures were built and expanded by various rulers, but the shrine suffered destruction multiple times, notably by Ghaznavid Sultan Mahmud and later during Mongol invasions. The most significant periods of expansion and beautification occurred under the Safavid and Qajar dynasties. Shah Tahmasp I initiated major renovations, including the construction of the golden dome and numerous iwans. Shah Abbas I, a devout Safavid ruler, walked from Isfahan to Mashhad on pilgrimage, further solidifying the shrine's importance and investing heavily in its development. Later, Nader Shah Afshar and Qajar kings like Fath-Ali Shah also contributed significantly, adding minarets, courtyards, and intricate tilework. In the 20th and 21st centuries, the complex has undergone continuous expansion and modernization, incorporating vast new courtyards, museums, libraries, universities, and hospitals, making it a sprawling city within a city dedicated to religious, cultural, and social services. Its enduring legacy is a testament to the devotion and reverence for Imam Reza.
