Story of this place

The British Museum, founded in 1753, holds the distinction of being the world's first national public museum. Its genesis lies in the will of Sir Hans Sloane, a prominent physician and naturalist, who bequeathed his vast collection of over 71,000 objects‚Äîencompassing books, manuscripts, natural history specimens, and antiquities‚Äîto the nation for a sum of ¬£20,000. Parliament subsequently passed an Act to establish the museum and house Sloane's treasures, opening its doors to "all studious and curious persons" in 1759 at Montagu House in Bloomsbury. From its inception, the museum was conceived as a universal institution, dedicated to collecting objects from around the globe to narrate the comprehensive story of human culture and natural history. Over the centuries, the museum's collection expanded dramatically through exploration, archaeological excavations, and generous donations, acquiring iconic items such as the Rosetta Stone (1802), the Parthenon Sculptures (Elgin Marbles, 1816), and the Egyptian Mummies. This rapid growth necessitated new premises, leading to the construction of the iconic present building, designed by Sir Robert Smirke in the Greek Revival style, largely completed by the mid-19th century. Its sprawling collection became so extensive that several departments eventually spun off: the natural history specimens formed the British Museum (Natural History) in 1881 (now the Natural History Museum), and the library collection became the British Library in 1973. Today, the British Museum remains a global repository of human history, art, and culture, continuously evolving its presentation of the world's diverse heritage. It continues to be a site of ongoing research, conservation, and education, drawing millions of visitors annually who come to marvel at its unparalleled breadth of human achievement and ingenuity across continents and millennia, from prehistoric tools to modern ethnography, all under one roof.
