Rockefeller Center, New York, USA

Highlights

Did you know?

It was the largest private building project ever undertaken in the United States at the time of its construction.

Did you know?

The complex is an iconic example of Art Deco architecture, featuring numerous murals, sculptures, and decorative elements throughout.

Did you know?

The famous Rockefeller Center Christmas Tree tradition started informally with construction workers in 1931; the official tree lighting ceremony began in 1933.

Did you know?

Radio City Music Hall, part of Rockefeller Center, was the largest indoor theater in the world when it opened in 1932.

Did you know?

The 'Top of the Rock' observation deck offers unparalleled 360-degree views of New York City, including Central Park and the Empire State Building.

Story of this place

Rockefeller Center, a monumental "city within a city," rose during the depths of the Great Depression, an audacious vision by John D. Rockefeller Jr. Originally conceived in the late 1920s as the site for a new Metropolitan Opera House, the 1929 stock market crash led the Opera to withdraw. Undeterred, Rockefeller Jr. pressed forward with a massive commercial and entertainment complex, personally financing its construction at a staggering cost of over $250 million (equivalent to billions today). This ambitious undertaking, employing tens of thousands of workers, became a symbol of American resilience and ingenuity during economic hardship. Designed in the distinctive Art Deco style, the complex broke ground in 1931 and was largely completed by 1939. Its fifteen original buildings, centered around the iconic GE Building (now Comcast Building), housed NBC Studios, Radio City Music Hall, international commerce, and vast office spaces. Architects Raymond Hood, Wallace Harrison, and others meticulously planned every detail, from the grand plazas and pedestrian promenades to the intricate bas-reliefs and public art, including Paul Manship's famous Prometheus statue. Rockefeller Center quickly established itself as a vibrant hub for business, media, and entertainment. Radio City Music Hall became a legendary venue, and the annual lighting of the Christmas Tree, first held informally by construction workers in 1931 and officially in 1933, transformed the Plaza into a global holiday spectacle. Over the decades, it has evolved from a commercial enterprise into a beloved cultural landmark, continually drawing millions of visitors who come to admire its architecture, ice skate in the plaza, enjoy world-class entertainment, and take in panoramic city views from Top of the Rock. Its legacy endures as a testament to grand urban planning and a beacon of New York City's enduring spirit.
