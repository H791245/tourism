Red Square, Russia

Highlights

Did you know?

The name 'Red Square' (Krasnaya Ploshchad) originated from the Old Russian word 'krasnaya,' meaning 'beautiful,' not 'red' in the political sense.

Did you know?

Lenin's Mausoleum, located on Red Square, has housed the embalmed body of Vladimir Lenin since 1924, attracting millions of visitors over the decades.

Did you know?

St. Basil's Cathedral, with its distinctive multi-colored onion domes, stands at one end of Red Square. Legend says Ivan the Terrible blinded its architects so they could never build anything as beautiful again, though this is likely apocryphal.

Story of this place

Red Square, or Krasnaya Ploshchad in Russian, holds a monumental place in Russian history, acting as the vibrant heart of Moscow for centuries. Its origins trace back to the late 15th century, when Tsar Ivan III cleared a large open space outside the eastern walls of the Kremlin. This clearance served practical purposes: to create a marketplace for merchants and to establish a firebreak, as wooden buildings were a constant fire hazard. Initially known by various names, it acquired its enduring title, "Krasnaya Ploshchad," in the 17th century. While often associated with communism, the word 'krasnaya' in Old Russian meant 'beautiful,' and only later took on the meaning of 'red.' Throughout its history, Red Square has witnessed countless pivotal events. It served as a bustling commercial hub, a venue for public proclamations, coronations of Tsars, and even public executions. During the Soviet era, it transformed into a symbol of state power, hosting grand military parades, most notably the May Day and Victory Day parades, showcasing the might of the Soviet Union. Lenin's Mausoleum, housing the embalmed body of Vladimir Lenin, was constructed here in 1924, becoming a central pilgrimage site for communists worldwide. Following the collapse of the Soviet Union, Red Square transitioned from a symbol of ideological might to a vibrant cultural and tourist destination. While still occasionally hosting military parades, it now frequently hosts concerts, ice-skating rinks in winter, and other public events. Flanked by iconic structures like the Kremlin, St. Basil's Cathedral, the State Historical Museum, and the GUM department store, Red Square remains a powerful testament to Russia's rich and complex past, a place where history continues to unfold daily. It was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1990.
